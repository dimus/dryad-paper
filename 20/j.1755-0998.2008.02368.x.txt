Isolation and characterization of 145 polymorphic microsatellite loci for the common frog (Rana temporaria)
CHIKAKO MATSUBA* and JUHA MERILÄ
Ecological Genetics Research Unit, Department of Biological and Environmental Sciences, University of Helsinki, PO Box 65, FI-00014 Helsinki, Finland
Abstract
We describe primers and polymerase chain reaction conditions to amplify 145 di-, triand tetranucleotide microsatellite loci from the common frog (Rana temporaria), a species commonly used as a model in ecological and evolutionary research. Primers were tested on 46 individuals from two Fennoscandian populations and yielded an average of six to nine alleles per locus (range = 1–30) depending on the population. Average observed heterozygosities in the two populations were 0.16 (range = 0–0.91) and 0.36 (range = 0–1).
Keywords: amphibian, microsatellites, primer, Rana temporaria, Ranidae Received 21 July 2008; revision accepted 22 August 2008
Blackwell Publishing Ltd
The common frog (Rana temporaria) is the most widespread amphibian species in Europe and a popular subject of ecological (e.g. Loman 2004; Pahkala et al. 2001), evolutionary (e.g. Laugen et al. 2003; Van Buskirk & Arioli 2005), and genetic studies (e.g. Hitchings & Beebee 1997; Palo et al. 2003; Johansson et al. 2007). At present, allozyme (e.g. Hitchings & Beebee 1997) and amplified fragment length polymorphism (Bonin et al. 2006) markers aside, the availability of nuclear genetic markers for the common frogs is as yet rather limited with only 26 microsatellite markers developed thus far (Berlin et al. 2000; Rowe & Beebee 2001; Pidancier et al. 2002). Here, we report 145 new microsatellite markers useful for kinship, population genetics and genomic investigations of common frogs.
A microsatellite library was developed from genomic DNA of a male common frog originating from Härnösand (62°37′N; 17°59′E), Sweden, using the SuperSNX linker method of Glenn & Schable (2005). Genomic DNA from liver tissue (kept in –20 °C) was extracted with phenol/ chloroform method (Sambrook et al. 1989) and eluted with distilled water (approximately 400 ng/μL) after 70% ethanol rinse. DNA was fragmented with two restriction enzymes (RsaI and XmnI; New England Biolabs) and ligated with the SuperSNX linker. DNA fragments containing microsat-
ellite sequences were captured with the aid of biotin beads and three oligo mixes biotinylated at 3′-position [Mix2: (AG)12 (TG)12 (AAC)6 (AAG)8 (AAT)12 (ACT)12 (ATC)8; Mix 3: (AAAC)6 (AAAG)6 (AATC)6 (AATG)6 (ACAG)6 (ACCT)6 (ACAT)6 (ACTG)6; Mix 4: (AAAT)8 (AACT)8 (AAGT)8 (ACAT)8 (AGAT)8]. The library was enriched once by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using the SuperSNX linker site. After the enrichment, PCR fragments were subcloned into pDrive plasmids using the PCR Cloning Kit (QIAGEN). White colonies were then picked into 12 96-well plates, and incubated in Luria-Bertani media. After growth, the plasmid DNA was prepared using the CosMCPrep kit (Agencourt), and both strands were sequenced using the BigDye Terminator cycle sequencing kit version 3.1 with vector primers T7 and SP6. Terminator removal was carried out using CleanSEQ (Agencourt) and a Biomek NXp biorobot (Beckman Coulter Inc.). Purified sequencing reactions were run on an ABI 3730 Automated DNA Sequencer (Applied Biosystems). All sequences were trimmed using Phred (Ewing & Green 1998; Ewing et al. 1998) before assembly and editing in Gap4 (Bonfield et al. 1995). Altogether, 737 clone sequences were obtained. Homology of obtained sequences to those already developed for this species was checked by using blast search provided by National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI). Furthermore, identical clones within the obtained sequences were removed from primary candidates. Of the 209 loci screened, 64 loci were discarded because of unclear or multiple peaks, or because of lack of polymorphism in both populations. One hundred and forty-five loci were successfully genotyped and the primers and characteristics of the loci are given in Table 1. The allele number, observed and expected heterozygosities as well as null allele probabilities for each locus were calculated with the program Cervus (Kalinowski et al. 2007). Deviations
from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium were tested with GenePop (Raymond & Rousset 1995).
The results are summarized in Table 2. Five of the loci were monomorphic in the Kilpisjärvi population, but none in the Tvedöra population. The number of alleles per locus ranged from one to 19 (mean = 5.99) in the Kilpisjärvi, and from two to 30 (mean = 9.10) in the Tvedöra population (Table 2). The expected heterozygosity ranged from 0 to 0.950 (mean = 0.302) for the Kilpisjärvi and from 0 to 1.000 (mean = 0.715) for the Tvedöra population (Table 2). Thirtynine (Kilpisjärvi) and 55 (Tvedöra) loci displayed significant deviations from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium (P < 0.1), but after table-wide Bonferroni correction, only 11 and 31 of these deviations were significant (Table 2).
