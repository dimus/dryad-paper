Abstract.—The use of comparative methods to test evolutionary hypotheses has become more common at both the macroand microevolutionary levels. The application of such techniques is especially troublesome at the interface of these levels because phylogenetic relationships are often difficult to estimate. The use of a technique developed to estimate intraspecific cladograms combined with more traditional methods of phylogenetic estimation can improve the estimate in data
sets containing a range of diversity when the lower bound of the range approaches 0% divergence.
For nucleotide sequence data from the 16S region of the mitochondrial DNA from 72 individuals representing 37 species, this combined-procedures approach improved the estimate of phylogenetic relationships using maximum-parsimony, maximum-likelihood, and neighbor-joining methods. The estimated trees were used to examine systematic hypotheses relating to the crayfish genus Orconectes and species relationships within the subgenus Procericambarus. The monophyly of Procericambarus is not supported by the mitochondrial data, and the hypotheses of unique origins of various morphological features previously used in determining crayfish relationships is unsupported. [Crayfish; mitochondrial DNA; molecular systematics; parsimony; phylogeny reconstruction; Orconectes.]
Estimated phylogenies have become instrumental in determining systematic relationships and classifications (de Queiroz and Gauthier, 1990) and in testing systematic hypotheses and exploring evolutionary trends (Harvey and Pagel, 1991). As the phylogenetic approach to studying various aspects of evolutionary biology becomes more widespread, the accuracy of these phylogenetic hypotheses becomes more important. Templeton et al. (1992) developed a new method for reconstructing evolutionary relationships based on a population genetic model of molecular evolution. Crandall (1994) showed that this method is more accurate than more traditional methods (e.g., maximum parsimony) for inferring evolutionary relationships when few characters are available. Templeton et al. (1992) suggested that a combination of traditional methods and this new method can be a powerful approach to estimating phylogenies when the range of nucleotide sequence variation within a data set is great and the lower bound ap-

Fitzpatrick (1987) put forth a phylogenetic hypothesis for the subgenera within Orconectes (see Fig. 6), yet hypotheses for species relationships within subgenera have not been proposed because of the paucity of cladistic characters. In our study, we examined the relationships within the subgenus Procericambarus. This subgenus contains 24 described species distributed mainly in the Ozark Plateau, Ouachita Province, and Cumberland Plateau (Fig. 1). These species are ecologically diverse, inhabiting lakes, ponds, and different areas within streams. They are also morphologically diverse; Orconectes macrus
(the Neosho midget crayfish) adults range from 2.3 to 5.1 cm in length, and O. longidigitus (the long-pincered crayfish) obtain lengths of 20 cm or more. The distributional areas of many of these species are well known, based on the work of Williams (1954b) and Pflieger (1987). Variation in distributional area ranges from extremely localized, e.g., O. saxatilis, which is known only from Pigeon Creek in LeFlore County, Oklahoma, to widespread, e.g., O. rusticus, which is distributed throughout much of the range of the subgenus itself (see Fig.
1).
With its diversity in ecology, morpholo-
gy, and distribution, this group provides an excellent opportunity to explore the association of this diversity with cladogenic events. To utilize this wealth of diversity in evolutionary studies, however, the phylogenetic relationships of the species must first be ascertained (Felsenstein, 1985). In this study, we estimated the phylogenetic relationships of the species within the subgenus using nucleotide sequence data from the 16S region of the mtDNA. We examined the monophyly of taxonomic species and species groups. When deviations from species monophyly were apparent, we explored possible explanations for these deviations. Insertion and deletion gaps were coded as missing characters and recoded as single events for phylogenetic analysis using parsimony (as suggested by Swofford, 1993). In maximum-likelihood and distance analyses, gaps were treated as missing data. The resulting data matrix for the 16S gene region is given in the Appendix. Parsimony analyses also were run with the insertion/deletion events deleted from the data matrix to determine their effects on phylogeny estimation.
Phylogenetic signal within the data set was assessed using the ga statistic of the random tree distribution (Hillis and Huelsenbeck, 1992). Phylogeny reconstruction was carried out using three alternative methods and combinations of these methods with the method of Templeton et al. (1992). Parsimony analyses were performed using the heuristic search option in PAUP with random addition of taxa (Swofford, 1993). To check for the possibility of multiple tree islands (Hendy et al., 1988; Maddison, 1991), at least 100 random addition searches were made for each alternative weighting scheme. A step matrix weighting transversions to transitions by 2:1 was used for the 16S data set because these data showed a significant bias towards transitional substitutions. The ratio of —2:1 was obtained by calculating the probability of change according to the model of Hasegawa et al. (1985). These probabilities were converted to weights using Felsenstein's (1981b) conversion. This bias is typical of the 16S region (Moritz et
al., 1987). The neighbor-joining method (Saitou and Nei, 1987) was used as implemented in ME—TREE (Rzhetsky and Nei, 1992) using the Jukes and Cantor (1969) and Kimura (1980) models of nucleotide substitution. The maximum-likelihood technique was implemented by the DNAML program in PHYLIP (Felsenstein, 1981a, 1991) .
These traditional methods were also used in conjunction with the method of Templeton et al. (1992). When sequences differ by few characters, this method is statistically powerful, which is not the case
with traditional methods. This power is obtained by conditioning the probability of a parsimonious connection between operational taxonomic units (OTUs) by the number of nucleotides shared between OTUs. Thus, for a pair of OTUs that differ by few nucleotides and share many, the probability of a parsimonious connection is greater than that between a pair of OTUs that differ by many nucleotides and share few. By combining the method of Templeton et al. with traditional methods and through the use of topological constraints on relationships established by the Templeton et al. method, one capitalizes on the statistical power of each method. The method of Templeton et al. indicated that parsimonious connections between pairs of OTUs were statistically justified for pairs separated by a maximum of 10 nucleotide substitutions (i.e., Templeton et al.,
1992: eq. 8; the probability of parsimony fell below 0.95 with 11 mutational steps). Therefore, for the combined analyses with each of the methods listed above, primary relationships were established with the method of Templeton et al. for those pairs of OTUs that differed by <10 characters, and these primary relationships were used as constraints for estimating the remaining tree structure. In most cases, the resulting networks were pairs of OTUs and were easily connected to the main tree. Those networks with more than two OTUs were connected to the main tree using arguments from coalescent theory. Donnelly and Tavare (1986) suggested that older alleles in a population are represented in higher frequency and with greater numbers of mutational connections than are younger alleles. This prediction was supported empirically (Crandall and Templeton, 1993) and through computer simulation studies (Castelloe and Templeton, 1994).
Confidence in the resulting relationships was assessed using the bootstrap procedure (Felsenstein, 1985, 1988; Felsenstein and Kishino, 1993; Hillis and Bull, 1993) for all methods except the Templeton et al. (1992) method. Here, probabilities were assigned using equation 8 of Templeton et al.

This test counts the number of mutational changes supporting topology A versus those supporting topology B and compares the relative levels of support against a binomial distribution with the null hypothesis of p = 0.50, i.e., equal support for either topology. Skewness has been established as a good indicator of phylogenetic signal (Hillis, 1991; Huelsenbeck, 1991; Hillis and Huelsenbeck, 1992). The addition of constraints to the phylogeny typically decreases the amount of phylogenetic signal in a data set (see Hillis, 1991, for examples). However, constraining relationships decreases the number of effective OTUs, thus tending to reduce the g1 statistic. Therefore, ga scores may not be directly comparable.
A test of the accuracy of the Templeton et al. (1992) method relative to maximum parsimony indicated that when few characters are available for analysis, the method of Templeton et al. greatly outperforms maximum parsimony in terms of making accurate connections between OTUs (Crandall, 1994). There is no way to prove that the combined-procedures approach has resulted in a more accurate estimate of the crayfish phylogeny because the true phylogeny is unknown. However, these three lines of evidence all indicate that the combined-procedures approach does result in a better estimate of phylogenetic relationships.
By combining the method of Templeton et al. (1992) with the maximum-likelihood approach, we were able to obtain a maximum-likelihood estimate. The entire unconstrained data set was too large for the maximum-likelihood algorithm. Clearly, the ability to obtain an estimate is a great improvement over no estimate at all. In combination with the neighbor-joining algorithm, the resulting tree had greater resolution at more basal nodes than did the tree estimated by neighbor joining alone, perhaps because of the increase in phylogenetic signal indicated above. Thus, the combined use of the method of Templeton et al. with the three major algorithms for estimating phylogenetic relationships improved the resulting estimate in each case, although in different ways. Huelsenbeck and Hillis (1993: fig. 7, analyses I, III) demonstrated that distance methods and parsimony methods tend to perform poorly when few characters are available for anal-
ysis. Using the method of Templeton et al. for clades that contain OTUs differing by few characters combined with a more traditional approach for those clades or taxa differing by more characters allows for an improved estimate of phylogenetic relationships. These estimated relationships were used to test systematic hypotheses relating to the crayfishes.
Testing Systematic Hypotheses
Monophyly of taxa.—Monophyletic groupings are important in a classification of organisms based on phylogeny (de Queiroz and Donoghue, 1990; de Queiroz, 1992) and in some species concepts (Cracraft, 1983, 1989). Therefore, it is important to explore the monophyly of taxonomic groups in a systematic study. Monophylet-
ic groupings were tested statistically by constraining the parsimony search to form monophyletic groups of the taxa being tested. The resulting tree was then compared with the most-parsimonious tree lacking the enforced constraint (i.e., that tree given in Fig. 3). Nonmonophyly was observed in 9 of the 24 species for which more than one sample was obtained. Of these nine cases of nonmonophyly, five were shown to be statistically significant, that is, the alternative constrained tree had significantly more character change associated with it than did the most-parsimonious tree (Table 4).
One explanation for the nonmonophyly of mtDNA haplotypes within a species is the problem of gene trees reflecting species (or population) trees (Pamilo and Nei, 1988). Watterson (1985) studied the problem of genetic divergence in two populations and effects of mutation and genetic drift on the probability that the samples form a monophyletic group. Padmadisastra (1987) extended those results to the three-population case and included the effects of population bottlenecks. These studies assumed complete isolation between populations at the time of divergence. Many models of speciation stipulate limited gene flow between ancestral and derived populations during the divergence of the two populations. Takahata and Slat-

Their results indicate that for some time after the divergence of two or more populations, there is a high probability that these populations will show a nonmonophyletic gene genealogy; therefore, the gene genealogy will not accurately reflect the population divergence. Neigel and Avise (1986) and Takahata and Slatkin (1990) have shown that the expected progression of gene genealogy following divergence is polyphyletic —> paraphyletic —» monophyletic. Many empirical examples of this phenomenon have recently been reported (Powell, 1991; Patton and Smith, 1994). The probability of monophyly is significantly reduced by the presence of gene flow (Takahata and Slatkin, 1990), increases with smaller population size (Padmadisastra, 1987; Pamilo and Nei, 1988), and is affected by the geographic distribution of the populations (Neigel and Avise, 1986). Given these results and the apparent recent divergence of the crayfish populations in this study (as indicated by the relative lack of genetic divergence among individuals), patterns of nonmonophyly may merely reflect this recent divergence and the discordance between 16S mtDNA genealogy and the species phylogeny. SUMMARY
A combination of methods for phylogenetic inference were used to estimate phylogenetic relationships among various crayfish species. The method of Templeton et al. (1992) was designed for intraspecifk divergences and has its greatest statistical power when there are many similarities and few differences in a data set of restriction sites or nucleotide sequences. More traditional methods of phylogeny reconstruction have their greatest statistical power when the number of differences are great and similarities are few (Huelsenbeck and Hillis, 1993). This analysis demonstrates the effectiveness of sequentially combining these strengths to estimate relationships among taxa that span this diversity of variation. When combined with maximum parsimony, the method of Templeton et al. greatly reduces the number of most-parsimonious trees and finds trees of shorter overall length, increases the amount of phylogenetic signal within the data set, and therefore provides a better estimate of phylogenetic relationships.
